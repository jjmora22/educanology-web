# Educanology Editorial Agent Plan

## 1. Purpose of the Editorial Agent

The Educanology editorial agent is a human-approved editorial workflow for producing one high-value article per working day, a ready-to-publish LinkedIn version and consistent AI-generated visual assets.

The system is not a generic blog generator. It should support the full editorial chain:

1. Topic or news detected
2. Research and source validation
3. Topic proposal with links for human validation
4. Human selection of the angle
5. Blog article draft
6. LinkedIn draft
7. Visual brief
8. AI image generation for blog
9. Optional AI image adaptation for LinkedIn
10. Review by Daniel or Juan José
11. Approval
12. Blog publication
13. LinkedIn publication, initially manual or approval-based

The first version should be safe, structured and human-controlled. It should produce drafts and visual briefs, not publish automatically.

## 2. Strategic Editorial Positioning for Educanology

Educanology should be positioned as an institutional, practical and implementation-oriented partner for education transformation.

Core positioning:

- Responsible AI in education with human supervision, pedagogical purpose and ethical use.
- Active learning, teacher training and meaningful student engagement.
- FabLabs, MakerSpaces and STEAM labs designed around pedagogy, territory and employability.
- Municipal education strategy linked to lifelong learning, participation and local innovation.
- European and public funding aligned with real local strategy, not isolated opportunistic applications.
- Implementation capacity: moving from vision, policy and equipment to adoption, governance, training, operations and measurable public value.

The editorial tone should be:

- Institutional
- Useful
- Strategic
- Practical
- Clear
- Portuguese from Portugal
- Confident without exaggerated claims
- Educational rather than promotional

The system must avoid claims such as "the best", "leading", "guaranteed" or unsupported results.

## 5. Guided Dialogue Workflow

The first implementation should be a guided dialogue, not an autonomous publishing system.

Daily start:

1. Determine the editorial category for the current working day.
2. The editorial agent must use current web search based on credible, verified sources, or approved source feeds, when proposing news-based or funding-based topics. It should not rely only on internal knowledge for recent information.
3. Propose several possible topics.
4. Include source links for each topic.
5. Clearly label confirmed facts, interpretation and possible Educanology angle.
6. Ask Daniel or Juan José to select or approve the topic and angle.
7. Do not write the final article until a human selects or approves the topic.

Topic proposal format:

```md
## Proposed topic

Title idea:

Category:

Why this matters:

Confirmed facts:

Interpretation / editorial opportunity:

Suggested Educanology angle:

Sources:
- Source name: URL
- Source name: URL

Recommended reviewer:
Daniel / Juan José
```

## 6. Daily Workflow From Discovery to Publication

1. **Discovery**
   - Check weekday category.
   - Search official sources, institutional publications, funding portals and reputable sector news.
   - Identify 3-5 possible topics.

2. **Validation**
   - Verify facts with primary sources where possible.
   - Avoid relying only on secondary media coverage.
   - Flag uncertainty.

3. **Proposal**
   - Present topic options with links.
   - Explain why each topic fits Educanology.
   - Ask for human selection.

4. **Selection**
   - Human selects topic and angle.
   - Human may assign voice: Daniel, Juan José or combined.

5. **Drafting**
   - Generate blog article in Portuguese from Portugal.
   - Generate LinkedIn post.
   - Generate visual brief and image prompt.

6. **Visual generation**
   - Generate blog hero image using the approved visual system.
   - Optionally generate/adapt LinkedIn image.

7. **Review**
   - Daniel or Juan José reviews facts, positioning and tone.
   - Agent revises if needed.

8. **Approval**
   - Human marks content as approved.

9. **Publication**
   - Blog publication may eventually be automated after approval.
   - LinkedIn publication should initially be manual or approval-based.

## 7. Topic Discovery and Validation Process

Preferred source types:

- European Commission pages
- EU funding portals and programme pages
- Portuguese government and public administration websites
- Municipal and intermunicipal publications
- Education ministry publications
- OECD, UNESCO, European Schoolnet and similar institutional sources
- University or research centre publications
- Reputable education, technology and policy media
- Official event, consultation or programme pages

Validation rules:

- Prefer primary sources over commentary.
- Confirm publication dates.
- Confirm whether a funding opportunity is open, upcoming, closed or only announced.
- Do not infer eligibility from vague language.
- Do not present commentary as fact.
- Avoid rewriting copyrighted news articles too closely.
- When using news, summarize and interpret rather than reproduce.

## 8. Blog Article Structure

Recommended structure:

```md
Title

Summary / deck

Objective of the article

What the article seeks to explain

Guiding thesis or research questions

Context and confirmed facts

Analysis by 3 to 5 main axes

Findings or key implications for municipalities, schools, school groups or education organisations

Practical recommendations

Risks, cautions or points requiring verification

How Educanology can help

Sources
```

Article requirements:

- Portuguese from Portugal.
- Articles should be serious editorial analyses, not short notes, generic summaries or promotional posts.
- Suggested length: 1,200 to 1,800 words.
- Maximum length: up to approximately 2,500 words only if the topic requires deeper analysis.
- Do not add filler, repetition or irrelevant background just to reach length.
- Articles should maintain the reader's attention through clear structure, useful arguments, concrete implications and practical recommendations.
- Articles should have a clear Educanology editorial line: frank, friendly, positive, constructive, resolutive, empathetic, institutional and practical.
- Articles should help the reader understand what Educanology believes should be done and why.
- Tone should be confident but not arrogant, useful but not simplistic, strategic but not bureaucratic.
- Articles should take a clear position when appropriate, while distinguishing confirmed facts from Educanology interpretation.
- Clear distinction between confirmed information and Educanology interpretation.
- No legal, financial or commercial commitments.
- No unsupported statistics.
- Avoid generic introductory filler.
- Include concrete implications and next steps.

Each article should answer:

- Why does this topic matter now?
- What is confirmed by credible sources?
- What interpretation does Educanology make?
- What should decision-makers understand?
- What practical steps could they take?
- Where should they be cautious?
- How could Educanology support them?

## 10. Visual Identity Strategy

The system should not depend on manually searched stock images. It should generate AI images with a consistent Educanology editorial visual identity.

Common mother line:

- European, Iberian and Portuguese atmosphere.
- Municipal and public education contexts.
- Photographic, institutional and human.
- Strategic, grounded and realistic.
- Warm natural light, real public buildings, classrooms, libraries, labs, civic spaces and municipal environments.
- People should look natural, diverse and plausible, not fake stock-photo perfect.
- Technology should appear as part of real learning and public work, not as sci-fi spectacle.

Avoid:

- Exaggerated sci-fi aesthetics.
- Cartoon robots.
- Fake-looking perfect stock-photo children.
- Generic empty iconography.
- Chaotic collages.
- Images full of text.
- Artificial elements without educational or territorial context.
- Overly glossy corporate imagery.

## 13. Source and Citation Rules

- Use official or primary sources whenever possible.
- Include source URLs in topic proposals and final drafts.
- Do not fabricate links.
- Do not invent dates, statistics, eligibility rules or legal obligations.
- Label interpretation clearly.
- For news-based content, do not rewrite source articles too closely.
- Use short quotes only when necessary and attribute them.
- Prefer paraphrase and synthesis.

## 14. Funding-Opportunity Rules

Funding articles require extra caution.

Rules:

- Use official programme or call pages whenever possible.
- Confirm whether the opportunity is open, upcoming, closed or general.
- Include relevant source URLs.
- Clearly distinguish confirmed call information from Educanology interpretation.
- Do not promise approval, funding success or eligibility.
- Do not provide legal or financial commitments.
- Explain strategic fit, possible project types and preparation steps.
- State when human verification is required before action.

Funding article structure:

```md
What was published or identified

Who may be interested

What is confirmed

What still needs verification

Why it matters for education or municipalities
```

## 15. Human Approval Workflow

Approval steps:

5. Agent revises.

7. Human approves image.

## 16. Proposed Content Statuses

- `idea`: initial detected topic.
- `researched`: sources collected and checked.
- `proposed`: presented to human for selection.
- `selected`: human approved topic and angle.
- `draft`: blog, LinkedIn and visual brief generated.
- `pending_review`: awaiting Daniel or Juan José review.
- `approved`: approved for publication.
- `scheduled`: publication date/time set.
- `published`: published on blog and/or LinkedIn.
- `rejected`: not suitable or intentionally abandoned.

## 25. Educanology Editorial Doctrine: Human-first technology adoption

Educanology defends a gradual, pedagogical and human-first adoption of technology in education.

Technology should amplify human capabilities, not replace the development of foundational skills. A useful metaphor is technology as a bicycle for human capabilities: it extends reach, speed and autonomy, but it does not replace balance, direction or human effort.

This doctrine should guide articles, LinkedIn posts, visual briefs and topic selection, especially in AI and digital education content.

Core principles:

- Before using a calculator, learners should understand basic arithmetic operations.
- Before relying on computers for writing, learners should develop motor coordination, handwriting and basic written expression.
- Before using AI to produce answers, learners should develop reading comprehension, written expression, reasoning, questioning and critical thinking.
- AI should not make homework superficially easier. It should make learning more meaningful, challenging, personalized and motivating.
- Personalization may consider learner interests, progress, needs, motivation, prior knowledge and context.
- Personalization must avoid presenting unsupported fixed "learning styles" theories as scientific fact.
- Articles should distinguish evidence-based personalization from neuromyths about fixed learning styles.
- Technology adoption should be connected with teacher training, assessment, ethical use, data protection, human agency and gradual implementation.
- Teacher agency is central: teachers need training, time, examples, confidence and professional judgment to use technology well.
- Technology adoption should be proportional to learner development: tools should be introduced when they strengthen understanding, autonomy and inclusion, not when they bypass necessary cognitive effort.

Editorial tone for this doctrine:

- Clear
- Frank
- Friendly
- Positive
- Constructive
- Resolutive
- Empathetic
- Institutional
- Practical

The editorial line should not be anti-technology. It should be pro-human development. Technology is valuable when it helps teachers teach better, students think more deeply, schools include more learners and municipalities build stronger learning ecosystems.

At the same time, technology adoption should not be presented as inherently positive. Educanology should explain the conditions under which technology can improve learning and the risks that appear when tools replace cognitive effort too early.

Educanology articles should avoid generic neutrality. They should have a clear point of view and help the reader understand what Educanology believes should be done and why, while keeping a clear separation between confirmed facts and Educanology interpretation.

For AI in education, the doctrine should normally lead to the following editorial position:

- AI can support teachers and students, but it should not be treated as a replacement for reading, writing, calculation, reasoning or critical thinking.
- AI can personalize practice and feedback, but the personalization must be evidence-aware and should not rely on unsupported fixed learning-style categories.
- AI can make learning more motivating when it creates better questions, richer feedback, more relevant challenges and more timely support.
- AI can damage learning when it rewards superficial completion, removes productive struggle or hides the reasoning process.
- Schools and municipalities should begin with policies, pilots, teacher training, ethical guidelines, data protection and assessment criteria before scaling use.

Evidence note:

- For AI and digital education topics, use credible current sources such as UNESCO, OECD, European Commission, education ministries, universities and peer-reviewed research.
- Do not invent scientific claims, statistics, funding rules, legal obligations or learning-science conclusions.
- When discussing personalization, motivation or learning differences, distinguish confirmed evidence from interpretation.
- When mentioning learning styles, avoid treating fixed learning-style categories as established scientific fact.
- When sources are uncertain or general, mark them clearly and require human verification before publication.
